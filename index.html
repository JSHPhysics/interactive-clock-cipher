How to use

Step 1 of 6
What is a clock cipher?
A clock cipher maps letters onto positions around a clock face. To encode a message, replace each letter with its position number. To decode, reverse the process. Rotating the wheel shifts every letter to a different position, acting as the cipher key — both sender and receiver must agree on the rotation.
Position 1 → letter A
Position 2 → letter B
Position 3 → letter C
…and so on around the face
Step 2 of 6
Choose your alphabet
Use the language dropdown to configure the wheel for a full alphabet. The clock expands to fit any alphabet — from 22 letters (Hebrew) up to 33 (Russian). Click ↓ Fill to populate letters in standard order, or ⚄ Shuffle to randomise their positions for a stronger cipher.
English (26): A B C D E … Z
Russian (33): А Б В Г Д … Я
Greek (24): Α Β Γ Δ Ε … Ω
Or use Custom (12) for any 12-symbol set
Step 3 of 6
Assign letters manually
Each row in the Cipher Letters panel corresponds to one clock position. Type a letter into the box — the cursor advances automatically so you can fill all positions quickly. Drag any row by its ⠿ handle to swap two letters around the wheel.
1 [A]   2 [B]   3 [C]   4 [D] …
Letters appear live on the clock face as you type
Step 4 of 6
Rotate the wheel to set the key
Click and drag the clock face to spin it — it snaps to the nearest position on release. Use the ◀ ▶ buttons to step precisely one position at a time. The fixed numbers in the outer ring never move, so you can always read which letter sits under each position number. Hit ↺ Reset to return to 0°.
Rotate +2 steps with A–E assigned:
Position 1 → C   Position 2 → D   Position 3 → E …
The rotation offset is the shared secret key
Step 5 of 6
Decode a message
Type (or paste) a sequence of position numbers into the Message box. Each number is instantly replaced by the letter sitting at that position on the current wheel. Spaces and punctuation pass through unchanged. The output updates live as you rotate the wheel, making it easy to try different keys.
Input: 3 1 20 5
Output: C A T E
(· shown for positions with no letter assigned)
Step 6 of 6
Encode a message
Type plain letters into the Message box instead. Any letter present on the wheel is replaced by its current position number. Letters not on the wheel are left unchanged. Rotating the wheel updates every number instantly — useful for comparing encodings at different key offsets.
Input: CAFE
Output: 3 1 6 5
Rotate the wheel — the numbers change automatically
